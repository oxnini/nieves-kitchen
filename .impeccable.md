## Design Context

### Users
Curious home cooks who browse globally as much as they cook. They use Nieves Kitchen on weekends and evenings on a laptop or tablet, often in a relaxed "what should I explore tonight?" mindset. The job is part practical (find a recipe to make), part imaginative (travel through food, collect a culinary journey via the passport feature). They are not beginners looking for hand-holding and not chefs looking for technique depth — they are enthusiastic generalists who want to feel guided, inspired, and quietly proud of what they've tried.

### Brand Personality
**Three words:** warm, considered, well-traveled.

The voice is that of a thoughtful friend who has collected recipes from trips, tucks them into a linen-bound book, and writes short notes in the margins. Confident but never loud. Generous with context (where a dish comes from, what makes it special) without turning into a blog essay. Editorial in craft, personal in voice.

Emotional goals: users should feel welcomed in (warm, inviting), taken seriously (considered, editorial), and subtly accomplished as their passport fills up.

### Aesthetic Direction
A blend of three references, in descending weight:

1. **Printed cookbook / editorial** (primary) — large serif display type, generous margins, drop caps and pull quotes where they earn their place, room for photography to breathe. Layouts reference how a well-designed cookbook handles hierarchy: confident headlines, quiet body text, intentional whitespace.
2. **Vintage passport & postal** (signature accents) — the passport feature leans into this directly: stamps, postmarks, ticket-stub perforations, ledger lines, embossed-looking seals, monospace date-stamps. Used as meaningful artifacts tied to the journey metaphor, NOT as decoration everywhere.
3. **Modern Nordic restraint** (discipline layer) — keeps the first two from becoming cluttered. Clean grids under the warmth, generous negative space, a tight palette actually used, no ornament for its own sake. This is what prevents "cookbook + passport" from tipping into kitsch.

**Theme:** Two warm modes, no true dark mode.
- **Parchment** (light, default): current `#FDF6EC` parchment palette with terracotta/turmeric/sage/teal accents. The signature feel.
- **Sepia** (warm-dark): a leather-bound-book variant — deep warm browns, cream text, gold/terracotta accents. Reads like a library at night, not a tech dashboard.

**Anti-references** (explicitly NOT this):
- Not SaaS / dashboard: no card-grid monotony, no neutral grays, no "productivity app" feel. Nieves Kitchen is a publication, not an admin panel.
- Not food-delivery: no high-contrast commerce UI, aggressive CTAs, coupon-bright colors, or stock-photo tiles. Curated, not transactional.
- Not rustic / kitsch: no chalkboard fonts, burlap textures, or country-kitchen "live laugh love" energy. Warm but never twee.

### Design Principles
1. **Publication, not product.** Treat every screen like a spread in a well-designed magazine: typographic hierarchy first, whitespace respected, photography given room. Interactive affordances are earned, not sprinkled.
2. **The passport is the soul.** Stamps, postmarks, and ledger details are the signature visual language, but they live inside the passport and journey moments — not sprayed across the whole app. Scarcity is what makes them feel collectible.
3. **Warm restraint.** The palette is warm; the composition is disciplined. Parchment, terracotta, turmeric, sage, teal are the recipe — use them with 60-30-10 intentionality, not evenly. Tint neutrals toward the warm hue; never use pure black or pure white.
4. **Typography does the heavy lifting.** A serif display face and a refined body face (Playfair + Inter is the current pairing — audit whether it still earns its place vs. a less ubiquitous alternative) handle hierarchy. Resist adding ornament to prop up weak typography.
5. **Delight at milestones, calm everywhere else.** Motion, confetti, and flourish are reserved for moments that genuinely matter (unlocking a region, earning a stamp, completing a journey). The rest of the app is quiet on purpose.
